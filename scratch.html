Introduction to Cats

Domestic cats (Felis catus) are among the most beloved and widely kept companion animals in the world — with an estimated 600 million cats living alongside humans globally.

Cats were first domesticated roughly 10,000 years ago in the Near East, where wildcats (Felis silvestris lybica) were drawn to early agricultural settlements by rodent populations. Over millennia, selective breeding and natural adaptation produced the extraordinary diversity of breeds, coat types, and temperaments we know today.

Unlike dogs, whose domestication dramatically reshaped their anatomy and behavior, cats retain many traits of their wild ancestors — making them one of the most behaviorally complex and independently-minded of all domestic animals.

Fast Facts

There are over 70 recognized cat breeds worldwide (TICA recognizes 73). The domestic cat can run up to 30 mph, has approximately 200 million odor receptors in its nose (humans have 5 million), and spends up to 16 hours a day sleeping.

How We Classify Cats

Cat breeds can be organized in several overlapping ways. Below are the major classification frameworks used by registries like The International Cat Association (TICA) and the Cat Fanciers' Association (CFA), each with notable breed examples.

  1. Classified by Coat Type
    • Short-haired — the most common; low maintenance grooming (e.g., Siamese, Abyssinian, British Shorthair)
    • Long-haired — flowing coats requiring daily brushing (e.g., Persian, Maine Coon, Norwegian Forest Cat)
    • Medium-haired — a balance between the two (e.g., Ragdoll, Siberian, Turkish Angora)
    • Hairless — little to no fur; require skin care and warmth (e.g., Sphynx, Peterbald, Donskoy)
    • Curly/Rex-coated — genetically wavy or kinked fur (e.g., Cornish Rex, Devon Rex, Selkirk Rex)
    • Wire-haired — stiff, crimped coat texture (e.g., American Wirehair)
  2. Classified by Body Type
    • Cobby — compact, round, heavily boned (e.g., Persian, Exotic Shorthair)
    • Semi-cobby — moderately compact (e.g., British Shorthair, American Shorthair)
    • Semi-foreign — moderate, slightly elongated (e.g., Abyssinian, Russian Blue)
    • Foreign/Oriental — slender, angular, long-limbed (e.g., Siamese, Cornish Rex, Oriental)
    • Long & Substantial — large, muscular, long body (e.g., Maine Coon, Norwegian Forest Cat)
  3. Classified by Geographic / Genetic Origin
    • Western breeds — developed primarily in Europe and North America (e.g., British Shorthair, Maine Coon, Ragdoll)
    • Eastern & South Asian breeds — originating in Asia (e.g., Siamese, Burmese, Japanese Bobtail, Bengal)
    • Middle Eastern & African breeds — closest to wildcat lineage (e.g., Egyptian Mau, Turkish Van)
    • Natural/Landrace breeds — evolved with minimal human selection (e.g., Norwegian Forest Cat, Siberian)
    • Hybrid breeds — crosses with wild felid relatives (e.g., Bengal from Asian Leopard Cat, Savannah from Serval)
  4. Classified by Temperament & Activity Level
    • High-energy / Playful — vocal and active; need stimulation (e.g., Bengal, Abyssinian, Siamese)
    • Calm & Lap cats — gentle and affectionate (e.g., Ragdoll, Scottish Fold, Persian)
    • Independent & Reserved — prefer their own space (e.g., Russian Blue, Norwegian Forest Cat)
    • Dog-like / Social — follow owners, enjoy company (e.g., Maine Coon, Birman, Tonkinese)
  5. Classified by Recognition Status
    • Fully recognized breeds — meet all standard requirements for showing
    • Provisional breeds — in the process of achieving championship status
    • Experimental breeds — newly developed; under evaluation
    • Domestic non-pedigree — mixed-breed or "moggy" cats (by far the most common)

What Differentiates Cat Breeds

At first glance, cats might all seem similar — but the differences between breeds span genetics, morphology, behavior, and health. Here are the primary axes of variation:

Characteristic Range / Examples Why It Matters
Size 5 lbs (Singapura) → 25+ lbs (Maine Coon) Affects space requirements, feeding, handling
Coat length Hairless (Sphynx) → extremely long (Persian) Determines grooming time and allergy sensitivity
Activity level Low (Ragdoll) → Very High (Bengal, Abyssinian) Determines enrichment and space needs
Vocality Silent (Russian Blue) → Very loud (Siamese) Important for apartment or shared-wall living
Sociability Independent (Norwegian FC) → Velcro (Burmese) Affects compatibility with children, other pets
Lifespan 8–10 yrs (some exotics) → 15–20 yrs (Siamese) Long-term commitment planning
Health predispositions HCM (Maine Coon), PKD (Persian), flat-face breathing issues (Exotic) Influences veterinary costs and screening

The "Mixed Breed" Advantage

Domestic mixed-breed cats (often called "moggies") tend to exhibit hybrid vigor — greater genetic diversity typically correlates with fewer inherited conditions, longer lifespans, and more variable — yet often robust — temperaments. They make up ~90% of cats kept as pets globally.
